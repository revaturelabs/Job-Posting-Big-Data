 The Ultimate Inside Job: You 
 December 30, 2016November 24, 2016  ~  K E Garland 
 Royalty Free Image 
 Spiritual growth is an inside job. That‚Äôs why I work on myself constantly. For me, inspiration stems from relationships and experiences within those relationships. For example, I‚Äôd noticed that people with the title  mother  oftentimes wrap their love in judgment. My  mother  -in-law, grand  mother  and step  mother  have all, at some point passed judgment on something they thought was best‚Ä¶for me. Whether it‚Äôs getting my oldest daughter‚Äôs hair done more frequently, not moving around so much or engaging with my dad in ways someone else saw fit, each of these women have offered unsolicited advice about how I choose to live. Conversely, I‚Äôd inherited a few of these traits myself. My younger cousins claimed I was ‚Äútoo judgmental‚Äù and my own daughter once said I was so ‚Äújudgy.‚Äù I probably was. What finally did it was a group conversation I had with a few friends. One thing led to another, and summer 2013, I decided to try and judge less. 
 It‚Äôs a lot harder than just saying it. 
 Think of judgment as a big box that encompasses many other things, such as superiority and arrogance. In order for me to stop passing judgment, I had to see myself as equal to everyone. I had to step down from my proverbial moral high ground and stop wagging my opinionated finger at others. We‚Äôre the same. I‚Äôm equal to the drug addicted, the shop-a-holic and the teenage mom. I‚Äôm not better than either of these people, thus I have zero right to judge their lives. If I‚Äôm feeling judgmental, then I remind myself of this:  anyone, at any moment could judge what you‚Äôre doing or have done in your life . Who am I to pass judgment on anyone‚Äôs life or life choices? 
 Image. ¬© 2016 K E Garland 
 My next project was learning to trust my intuition. I‚Äôve always had a good sense of how I felt, but somewhere along the way, I‚Äôd stopped fully listening. That is until I read T.D. Jakes‚Äô  Instinct . My husband and I were having some rough times and I‚Äôd met a friend to vent. I didn‚Äôt know what to do. She suggested we read the book together. Though I‚Äôm not religious, I am open to new ideas, so I agreed. I was so inspired by this book that I attempted a Facebook group centered on the ideas. That was a flop. But my renewed sense of following my heart was not. Using one‚Äôs instinct means consciously living life and being mindful about those pesky feelings. You must be perceptive and pay attention to that thing in the pit of your stomach that‚Äôs warning you about where you are and who you‚Äôre with. Though Bishop Jakes situates the concept in a discussion about passion and purpose, he also touches on relationships. He describes how people grow, sometimes together and sometimes apart due to monotony. Either way, instinct can show you how to proceed. I‚Äôd decided then and there to be quiet so I could hear. I quit a job that was too far to drive, wrote a book of Kwotes, started a blog, and just celebrated my 19th year of marriage. I firmly believe intuition is an underrated tool that we all have. 
 The last principle is a result of my father‚Äôs death. So I‚Äôm still figuring it out and listening for answers. When my dad died, I needed a lot more compassion and care than I thought I would. Because I had been following my intuition, I was in tune with my emotions. I requested empathy from specific people. It didn‚Äôt matter though. Considerations from them didn‚Äôt flow like I thought they would. I was very confused. All this time I thought that compassion was an easy sentiment to provide. It turns out that I was mistaken. Compassion is made up of three parts: (1) putting yourself in another person‚Äôs place, (2) imagining what she or he might be feeling and (3) doing something considerate. That‚Äôs a lot to ask of anyone. It‚Äôs a challenge. It takes extra effort. As it turns out, it‚Äôs something that I shouldn‚Äôt have sought out. So I stopped. Instead, I began showing other people compassion. Like I said, this one is a work in progress but already I feel better  being  compassionate, rather than seeking it. 
 ‚ÄúI‚Äôm not perfect.‚Äù We use this phrase often. But what does it mean? Does it mean that you stay stuck in your imperfect self, while asking forgiveness for bad behavior and judging other people‚Äôs perceived imperfections? I don‚Äôt have a universal answer. But I do believe that we can all be better than we were yesterday if we try. How are you willing to be a better you? What advice would you add to this?
