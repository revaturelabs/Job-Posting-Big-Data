temporary, seasonal, contract or per diem work.Natalie Surridge, program director for the Oxford Workforce Development Partnership, said over 50 per cent of those jobs were in the manufacturing sector.“We are in a unique situation in that we have a significant number of jobs — particularly in manufacturing but across all sectors — and a diminishing pool of labour to fill those jobs as the baby boomer generation retires,” Surridge said. “The partnership is continuing to support our business community in their efforts to secure the talent they require to thrive and is currently exploring a myriad of options to address the skill and labour gaps.”Unemployment rates have been consistently low across the entire region, she said.AdvertisementStory continues belowThis advertisement has not loaded yet, but your article continues below.Article content continued“It’s due to a strong local economy with a number of local and regional employers expanding at a rapid rate across a number of sectors,” she said.An employer survey from 2017 determined that top reason sighted by employers for why positions were hard to fill was because there were “not enough applicants,” Surridge said.And for the first time in a long time, she said, the Labour Force Survey showed a number of growth and open positions that don’t require post-secondary education.“This demand for employees will present new opportunities to groups that have been traditionally under-represented in the work force such as persons with disabilities, new Canadians, youth,” she said.Woodstock development officer Brad Hammond said all around the Friendly City there are signs of a stellar economy with new residential and industrial buildings going up in many locations.“Over the last year it has been very solid according to all the indicators we go by,” he said. “Our land sale activities have been very good.”Last year the city sold 30-acres of industrial land and they have sold at least 25 acres so far this year.It’s a number the economic department keeps a close eye on.“On average every acre we sell creates 10 new jobs,” Hammond said.By the end of July the city had issued $38 million worth of industrial
